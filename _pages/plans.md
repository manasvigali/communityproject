  <h1>Investment Plans — Your Roadmap to Financial Independence</h1>
  <p class="lead">Every investor starts somewhere. These roadmaps are designed for your age, budget, and goals. Pick the one that fits you and start building your financial future.</p>

  <div class="plans-intro">
    <div class="info-card">
      <h3>🎯 How to Use These Plans</h3>
      <p>Each plan is tailored for a specific age group with realistic starting amounts, risk levels, and step-by-step guidance. Follow the steps in order — they build on each other.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <!-- Plan 1: Teen Starter -->
  <section class="plan-card" id="plan-teen">
    <div class="plan-header">
      <span class="plan-age">Ages 14–17</span>
      <span class="risk-badge low">🟢 Low Risk</span>
    </div>
    <h2>Teen Starter — Build Your Foundation</h2>
    <div class="plan-meta">
      <span><strong>Starting Amount:</strong> $0–$50</span>
      <span><strong>Timeline:</strong> 2–3 years</span>
    </div>
    <p>This plan is for you if you're in high school, have little to no investing experience, and want to learn safely. Focus on education first — knowledge is your best investment right now.</p>

    <h3>Step-by-Step Roadmap</h3>
    <ol class="plan-steps">
      <li><strong>Open a Custodial Savings Account</strong> with a parent or guardian. Banks like Ally, Marcus, and Capital One offer competitive rates for teens. Even 2–3% annual interest beats under a mattress.</li>
      <li><strong>Start Paper Trading (Virtual Investing)</strong>. Use platforms like Investopedia's Stock Simulator or Webull's paper trading. You get $100,000 virtual cash — use it to practice buying/selling without any real risk.</li>
      <li><strong>Learn Compound Interest by Doing Math.</strong> If you save just $20/week ($80/month) at 7% annual return over 40 years, you'd have <strong>$236,000</strong>. But if you start at 15 instead of 25 — that same $80/month becomes <strong>$490,000</strong>. Time is your greatest advantage.</li>
      <li><strong>Join a Stock Market Simulation Contest.</strong> Many high schools and universities hold competitions. The Wall Street Simulator Challenge and Stock Game by the CFA Institute are great starting points. Compete with classmates — learning is more fun together.</li>
      <li><strong>Research One Company Per Month.</strong> Pick a company you use daily (Nike, Apple, Starbucks). Read their annual report summary on SEC.gov or their investor page. Write down: What do they sell? Who are their customers? Is the business growing?</li>
    </ol>

    <div class="plan-tips">
      <h4>💡 Pro Tips for Teens</h4>
      <ul>
        <li>Use allowance or birthday money to practice with real $1–$5 purchases of fractional shares</li>
        <li>Follow financial news on YouTube — channels like Khan Academy Finance and Ben Felix explain concepts clearly</li>
        <li>Never invest money you'll need for bills, books, or food within the next 6 months</li>
        <li>Tell your parents what you're learning — they can help guide you toward good choices</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Plan 2: Student Builder -->
  <section class="plan-card" id="plan-student">
    <div class="plan-header">
      <span class="plan-age">Ages 18–21</span>
      <span class="risk-badge medium">🟡 Medium Risk</span>
    </div>
    <h2>Student Builder — Grow Your Wealth</h2>
    <div class="plan-meta">
      <span><strong>Starting Amount:</strong> $50–$200/month</span>
      <span><strong>Timeline:</strong> 3–5 years</span>
    </div>
    <p>You're old enough to open your own brokerage account. This plan focuses on building consistent investing habits while you're in college — the period when small amounts grow into meaningful wealth.</p>

    <h3>Step-by-Step Roadmap</h3>
    <ol class="plan-steps">
      <li><strong>Open a Student Brokerage Account.</strong> Fidelity, Charles Schwab, and Vanguard all accept 18+ account holders. No parent needed. Compare: Fidelity has $0 minimums and fractional shares; Schwab offers excellent research tools.</li>
      <li><strong>Build Your Emergency Fund First.</strong> Before investing a dollar more, save $500–$1,000 in a separate high-yield savings account (5%+ APY at current rates). This prevents you from having to sell investments during emergencies.</li>
      <li><strong>Start Dollar-Cost Averaging into Index Funds.</strong> Set up automatic weekly transfers of $25–$50 into a low-cost S&P 500 ETF like VOO (Vanguard, expense ratio 0.03%) or VTI (Total Stock Market). Over 40 years, $100/month at 8% return = <strong>$476,000</strong>.</li>
      <li><strong>Route Part-Time Income to Investments.</strong> Got a campus job? Set up automatic transfer of 50% of your earnings to your investment account right when you get paid. If you earn $300/month, that's $150 automatically investing.</li>
      <li><strong>Allocate: 70% Index Funds / 20% Individual Stocks / 10% Learning Experiments.</strong> The 10% "learning fund" lets you pick fun stocks to research and watch — like a company related to your major. If it goes wrong, you only lost learning money, not your future.</li>
      <li><strong>Review Monthly, Rebalance Quarterly.</strong> Check portfolio once a month (don't obsess daily). Every 3 months, rebalance if any single holding grows beyond 10% of your total portfolio.</li>
    </ol>

    <div class="plan-tips">
      <h4>💡 Pro Tips for Students</h4>
      <ul>
        <li>Use your student discount — many brokerages offer reduced fees for students</li>
        <li>Join or start a campus investing club. Study groups make complex topics easier and more fun</li>
        <li>Take advantage of 401(k) matching if your university or internship offers one — it's free money</li>
        <li>Avoid margin (borrowed trading). As a student, you can't afford to lose what you haven't earned yet</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Plan 3: Future Investor -->
  <section class="plan-card" id="plan-future">
    <div class="plan-header">
      <span class="plan-age">Ages 22+</span>
      <span class="risk-badge moderate-high">🟡→🟠 Moderate-High Risk</span>
    </div>
    <h2>Future Investor — Build Lasting Wealth</h2>
    <div class="plan-meta">
      <span><strong>Starting Amount:</strong> $200–$500+/month</span>
      <span><strong>Timeline:</strong> 5+ years</span>
    </div>
    <p>You've graduated or are close to it. Time to scale up your investing strategy, diversify beyond stocks, and develop an investment philosophy that works for your goals and risk tolerance.</p>

    <h3>Step-by-Step Roadmap</h3>
    <ol class="plan-steps">
      <li><strong>Max Out Tax-Advantaged Accounts.</strong> If available, contribute enough to your employer's 401(k) to get full matching. Open a Roth IRA (contributions come in after-tax, but all growth is tax-free forever). In 2024, you can contribute up to $7,000/year ($8,000 if 50+).</li>
      <li><strong>Build a Diversified Portfolio.</strong> Classic allocation for young adults: 60% US Stocks (VTI/VOO) / 20% International Stocks (VXUS) / 15% Bonds (BND) / 5% Alternatives (gold, REITs). Adjust based on your risk tolerance — younger can afford more stock.</li>
      <li><strong>Explore Real Estate Crowdfunding.</strong> Platforms like Fundrise and RealtyMogul let you invest in real estate from $10. You earn monthly rental income distributions without being a landlord. Historically 8–12% annual returns.</li>
      <li><strong>Consider Crypto — But Only After the Foundation.</strong> If your core portfolio is solid, allocate ≤5% to Bitcoin or Ethereum. Think of it as venture capital: high risk, but if one project succeeds massively, the gains can be enormous. Never invest money you can't afford to lose completely.</li>
      <li><strong>Develop Your Investment Thesis.</strong> Write your own "investing memo" — what sectors do you understand? What trends do you see? How do you define value? Reading Buffett's annual letters, Peter Lynch's "One Up on Wall Street," and Ray Dalio's "Principles" will help shape your philosophy.</li>
      <li><strong>Mentor Younger Students.</strong> Teaching what you've learned reinforces your own knowledge. Help classmates understand the basics — the best way to learn is to explain.</li>
    </ol>

    <div class="plan-tips">
      <h4>💡 Pro Tips for Graduates</h4>
      <ul>
        <li>Inflation is your silent enemy. $1,000 saved under a mattress loses ~20% purchasing power in 10 years at 3% inflation</li>
        <li>Tax-loss harvesting: sell losing positions to offset gains — many investors skip this simple tax strategy</li>
        <li>Emergency fund should grow to 6 months of expenses, not just $500–$1,000</li>
        <li>Don't chase trends. The investors who get rich slowly are those who stay invested through every cycle</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </section>
